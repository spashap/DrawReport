You've probably seen articles claiming a drawing reveals a child's hidden fears, or that
a particular color "means" something is wrong. It's a tempting idea. It's also not how
children's drawing actually works.

## What a drawing is not

A single drawing is **not** a psychological or medical assessment. The color black doesn't
mean sadness. One dark picture doesn't reveal a hidden problem. Real child psychologists are
careful here for a good reason: reading emotions or diagnoses from a picture isn't supported
by solid evidence, and it leads parents to worry about the wrong things.

## What a drawing actually shows

What a drawing *does* show — clearly and usefully — is **skills**. How a child uses the page.
How confidently the line moves. Whether shapes hold together. How color is chosen and applied.
Whether there's a story. These are visible, real, and they develop in fairly predictable
stages as children grow (scribbling, pre-schematic, schematic, and then dawning realism).

## The honest way to read one

The approach we take is simple: tie every observation to something **you can actually see**
in the drawing, and read it against what's typical for the child's age. A confident outline,
a thoughtful layout, careful filling — these point to developing graphic skills, not to a
diagnosis. From there, the useful question isn't "what's wrong?" but "what's already going
well, and what could we gently try at home?"

That's the whole idea: a calm, educational look at what your child can do — not a verdict on
who they are.
