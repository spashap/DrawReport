It's one of the most common worries parents bring us: "For weeks my child has only used black.
Is something wrong?" The short answer: almost always, no.

## Why kids reach for one color

There are plenty of ordinary reasons. Black makes a strong, satisfying line. It's often the
marker that still works when the others have dried out. A child may be focused on **outlines**
right now — a normal stage where the shape of things matters more than filling them with color.
Sometimes it's simply the pen that was closest.

## What the color does not tell you

A color choice is not a mood reading. The idea that "black = sadness" or "dark = anxiety" is a
myth — it isn't supported by careful research, and treating it as a signal tends to create worry
where there's none. We never interpret a child's feelings from their palette.

## What to look at instead

If you're curious, look at the **skills**: Is the line confident? Are shapes holding together?
Is there a story? Those are the things a drawing genuinely shows. And if the single-color phase
has you wondering, the gentlest nudge is an invitation, not a correction: "Want to add who lives
in this house?" — and offer a few colors nearby, no pressure.

More often than not, the colors come back on their own.
